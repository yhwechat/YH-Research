When the "Buy Anything" Trade Stops

— And Why That's the Value Investor's Moment

▎IN BRIEF

The first half of 2026 was a triumph for U.S. equities: attach the words "artificial intelligence" to almost anything, and it rose. Yet in the holiday-shortened week before Independence Day, the trade that had defined the half suddenly broke — chip stocks shed more than a tenth of their value in two sessions. At Yong Hui, we see this not as the end of the AI story, but the opposite: a historic revolution, rivalling the internet itself, is only just beginning — and every bout of turbulence like this hands the prepared value investor a chance to own great businesses at fair prices.

1. The Half-Year Scorecard: Brilliance, and a Crack

The three major U.S. indices roared through the first half. The S&P 500 rose about 9.6%, the Nasdaq Composite about 12.8%, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average 8.9% — its best first half since 2021. The small-cap Russell 2000 surged nearly 22%, its strongest half since 1991.

The engine was almost entirely AI-related technology. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index (SOX) soared more than 80% in the first half. Chips, compute, data centres — anything within reach of a GPU was chased by capital.

Then, in the first week of July, the mood turned. The SOX fell across two consecutive sessions for a cumulative drop of about 12%; Micron alone fell more than 10% in a single day. Tellingly, the Dow set a fresh record in the very same week — capital was not fleeing AI, but repricing it, asking a sharper question: along this value chain, who will actually earn enough to justify today's prices?

For the value investor, that process of repricing is not a threat. It is a gift.

2. Our Core View: A Real, and Durable, Revolution

Before any word of caution, let us state our position plainly: Yong Hui is firmly optimistic about the AI revolution.

We believe it may prove the single largest wave of value creation of our era — a reshaping of productivity whose breadth and depth rival, and may exceed, what the personal computer and mobile internet delivered before it. This is not a slogan. It is visible in hard, audited profit.

And that is precisely what most distinguishes today from 2000. Back then, the technology companies leading the market were, in aggregate, destroying capital — Cisco traded near 200 times earnings, Pets.com had no earnings at all, and the entire story rested on a future yet to arrive. Today's leaders are among the most profitable enterprises in corporate history: Nvidia, for one, earned roughly US$117 billion in GAAP net income in fiscal 2026. The rubble left by a bursting bubble, and growth built on solid earnings, are two very different things.

So we read each pullback not as a siren of doom, but as a signal of opportunity. The question was never whether to participate in this revolution — only at what price, and through which businesses.

3. Echoes of History: A Great Technology vs. a Great Investment

To answer that question, history offers a mirror.

Rewind to 2000. The market's darling was Cisco — the networking giant laying the "plumbing" of the internet. At the peak of the frenzy, Cisco briefly became the most valuable company in the world, its market capitalisation surpassing US$500 billion. The title of its annual report that year carried a telling exhortation: "Capture the Momentum." Many did.

The internet did reshape the world as promised, and Cisco remains an excellent company to this day — yet its share price never returned to that 2000 peak in the two decades that followed.

Herein lies a rule value investors repeat like a mantra: a great technology and a great investment are never the same thing — the difference is price. One study spanning fifty years offers a sobering footnote: of stocks trading at 30-to-40 times sales, only about one in five outperformed the market over the following twelve months; extend the horizon to ten years, and that figure falls into the single digits.

In other words, being right about the direction of a revolution does not guarantee a profit — unless you also stay disciplined about the price you pay. And it is precisely market volatility that periodically restores prices to reason.

4. Four Signals: Reading This Capital Cycle

So what drives today's prices? Four news items from the past week together sketch the full arc of this AI capital-expenditure cycle — from demand to supply.

Signal 1 · The world's most cash-rich company can't get memory either

According to Bloomberg, the Financial Times and Nikkei, Apple is in talks to buy memory chips from two Chinese makers on a U.S. Defense Department list — ChangXin Memory (CXMT) and Yangtze Memory (YMTC) — for devices sold in China, with Tim Cook himself lobbying Washington. The symbolism is striking: a hardware giant sitting on vast cash reserves must seek new suppliers because AI data centres have all but drained the world's memory supply. Apple had already raised prices on Mac, iPad and Vision Pro lines citing an "unprecedented" surge in memory costs — notably excluding the iPhone, itself a demonstration of pricing power. This is the truest picture of just how hot the demand side of this cycle has become.

Signal 2 · Meta wants to sell its "excess" compute

Also this past week, Meta announced plans for a cloud business (internally "Meta Compute") to sell the surplus capacity of its AI infrastructure to outside customers. Meta shares jumped about 9% — investors finally saw a path for this company, with 2026 capex guided at roughly US$115–135 billion, to monetise its enormous outlay. Yet the same news sent two "neocloud" providers, CoreWeave and Nebius, down about 12% on the day. The market's reaction was pointed: the moment a hyperscaler says it has "excess" compute to sell, the supply side of the ledger is suddenly re-examined.

Signal 3 · Masayoshi Son's 10-gigawatt bet

Next, SoftBank Group announced a U.S. neocloud venture, "SB Neo," planning to draw ultimately on as much as 10 gigawatts of energy and AI infrastructure. SoftBank's cumulative investment in OpenAI — around US$65 billion — gives the new business a ready anchor customer. And this was merely the tip of the iceberg: in the span of ten days, the market saw four large neocloud deals. More intriguing still is where the money comes from — Nvidia has begun offering credit support to its own customers, letting cloud providers deploy its GPUs without shouldering the full capital cost. This "supplier-financing-the-customer" circularity faintly echoes Cisco's own "vendor financing" of telecom clients a generation ago — something every investor should note.

Signal 4 · Korea's factory-building frenzy

At the very top of the supply chain, the gates of supply are creaking open. Coordinated by the Korean government, Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix have launched an investment plan of unprecedented scale, aiming to build four to five new fabs and to double DRAM capacity within five years. Samsung's P5 plant in Pyeongtaek is expected to come online around 2028; SK Hynix's M15X is slated for mid-2027.

Threaded together, these four signals trace a clear logic: Apple scrambling for supply (demand) at one end, and Meta's "surplus," SoftBank's bet and Korea's fabs (supply) at the other — the two ends of a single capital-expenditure cycle. And the cardinal lesson of capital cycles is this: today's shortage and tomorrow's glut are often two faces of the same coin. New capacity takes years to move from plan to production, and the scales of supply and demand swing in cycles — with each swing minting, in share prices, both the froth of overvaluation and the bargains of the overlooked.

5. What It Means: Holding to Value's Common Sense

Understanding all this, Yong Hui's stance follows naturally: we embrace the AI opportunity wholeheartedly — but we choose to embrace it as value investors. Three pieces of common sense matter especially now:

First, seek moats among the winners, not momentum in the crowd. This revolution will surely produce a cohort of epic, enduring businesses; but concentration is not conviction. When an index's gains rest heavily on a handful of stocks priced for perfection, telling those with truly durable moats from those merely riding the updraft is the value investor's central task.

Second, watch the return on capital expenditure, not its scale. The market often misreads "spending" as "growth." But what ultimately decides an investment's fate is whether those astronomical outlays convert into durable free-cash-flow returns. Grasp that, and you have a compass amid the noise.

Third, treat volatility as a friend. To the short-term trader, a pullback is risk; to the patient value investor holding cash, it is a window to buy exceptional businesses at fair prices. AI will almost certainly reshape industries as the internet did — and as Cisco in 2000 showed, being right on direction still requires being right on price. The market's periodic panics are exactly the force that makes prices attractive again.

The AI revolution is real and enduring, and the opportunity it opens may be the grandest our generation of investors will ever encounter. Of that, Yong Hui is convinced.

Yet history reminds us, again and again: the best opportunities rarely sit where the crowd is thickest, but in the corners momentarily out of favour — those with solid businesses and lasting moats. To stay optimistic without losing discipline, to embrace change without forgetting price — this is the value investor's edge across every cycle, and the source of ultimate success.

The great revolution has only just begun. So, too, has the opportunity that belongs to the prepared.
