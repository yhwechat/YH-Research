Warsh's First Meeting:
A Special Note on the Fed Decision
Weekly Market Insights · June 2026
The rate is all but decided. The real suspense is just beginning. This is Kevin Warsh's first meeting as Fed Chair — and a new chair known for saying less must now send his first signal into the headwind of three-year-high inflation.
01  Why This Meeting Is Different

On Wednesday afternoon (US Eastern Time), the Fed announces its latest rate decision — the first FOMC meeting chaired by Kevin Warsh, who was sworn in as the 17th Chair on 22 May. That alone makes it consequential.

Warsh served as a Fed governor from 2006 to 2011 and lived through the 2008 crisis. He is widely read as hawkish — a long-standing critic of the post-crisis balance-sheet expansion — and an advocate of a central bank that does less, including offering less forward guidance. With markets having little history to anchor on, that "less-is-more" style means every word he chooses can move prices. The communication itself is a source of volatility.

The deeper tension: Warsh was nominated as a relative dove, arguing that the AI boom would lift productivity and tame inflation. The data, on his very first day, has cornered him in the opposite direction.

02  The Backdrop: Boxed In by Inflation

May CPI rose to 4.2% year-on-year, the highest since April 2023 and the third straight monthly acceleration. Energy did most of the work — prices up roughly 23.5% over the year amid this year's geopolitical conflict and Strait of Hormuz disruptions.

But the picture isn't one-sided. Core CPI (ex-food and energy) rose just 0.2% on the month, half of April's pace, hinting that underlying pressure may be easing. Meanwhile the labour market refuses to soften: May payrolls came in at +172K against an +80K consensus, with unemployment steady at 4.3%.

Key context: The federal funds rate has held at 3.50%–3.75% since December 2025 — four straight holds. March's dot plot still implied one cut in 2026 (a year-end median near 3.4%). With inflation now hotter, markets have swung toward pricing a possible hike this year.
03  What to Expect: Three Things Beyond the Rate

The rate itself is close to a foregone conclusion — futures price roughly a 97% chance of no change. What actually matters sits around the decision:

① The dot plot. The consensus is that the Fed will remove its last projected cut, formally confirming the end of the easing cycle. Some houses expect at least three of the twelve voters to pencil in a hike for 2026.

② The statement language. Watch whether the Fed drops its "easing bias" — not a hike, but a clear signal the committee no longer assumes rates only go down.

③ Warsh's debut. His first press conference — tone, framing of the inflation-versus-jobs balance, and how much guidance he offers — will set the template for his tenure.

04  Scenarios and Likely Market Reactions
Base case · Hawkish hold (most likely)
Rates unchanged; the dot plot drops the cut and the statement turns neutral, but Warsh stays balanced and avoids alarming markets. Reaction: mostly priced in — equities range-bound to mildly choppy, long-end Treasury yields edge up, the dollar firms modestly.
High-flying AI names: broadly steady, but with valuations leaning heavily on discounted future cash flows, they are unusually sensitive to any uptick in yields — expect wider swings than the broad index.
Hawkish surprise · Hike signalled
Multiple dots show hikes and the median shifts up, or Warsh sounds notably hawkish. Reaction: a sharp risk-off move — the S&P 500 could fall 2%+ intraday, growth stocks hit hardest while defensives outperform; yields spike across the curve and the dollar surges.
High-flying AI names: hit hardest. Long-duration, high-multiple AI leaders and "AI-themed" stocks typically see amplified drawdowns; the bigger the prior run-up and the more distant the earnings, the greater the downside — profit-taking can turn into a rush for the exits.
Dovish surprise · Easing bias kept
Warsh downplays inflation, leans on the AI-productivity thesis, and the dots keep a cut alive. Reaction: risk assets rally, growth stocks lead, yields fall and the dollar softens — though watch for markets questioning the Fed's inflation resolve.
High-flying AI names: the biggest beneficiaries. A lower discount rate plus Warsh personally endorsing the "AI lifts productivity" narrative could spark a fresh leg up in AI leaders — though the rally's durability is in doubt if markets fear inflation running loose.
05  What It Means for US Equity Investors

Through a value-investing lens, the short-term swings around any single meeting matter far less than the macro direction it reveals. If the easing cycle is over and rates stay "higher for longer," a structurally higher discount rate will keep testing richly-valued growth names — while businesses with durable cash flows, pricing power and disciplined capital allocation are best placed to ride through the rate cycle.

We treat Warsh's pivot as a re-calibration of risk pricing, not a timing signal. Rather than guessing the Fed's next move, we prefer to own genuinely excellent businesses at sensible valuations — that, ultimately, is what carries a portfolio through uncertainty.
