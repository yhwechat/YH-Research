As the Trillion-Dollar IPOs Line Up: Three Risks to the AI Mania

June 13, 2026  |  Yong Hui Research

Just yesterday (June 12), SpaceX debuted on the Nasdaq in the largest IPO in history, pushing retail enthusiasm worldwide to a fever pitch. But the acts the market truly awaits are the two that follow — Anthropic and OpenAI, AI giants valued near a trillion dollars, edging ever closer to the public markets. Trillion-dollar IPOs are lining up and euphoria is near its peak. Yet history reminds us that every cry of "this time is different" tends to end in strikingly familiar ways. We do not predict the timing of a bust — we examine where the cracks may spread from.

History as a Guide: The Capital-Cycle Script

Yesterday, SpaceX (Nasdaq: SPCX) began trading, opening at $150 per share — roughly 11% above its $135 IPO price — and raising a record $75 billion at an implied valuation of about $1.77 trillion, the largest IPO in Wall Street history. As the only one of the three companies to disclose audited financials, its prospectus is candid about the risks: its xAI unit is posting sizeable quarterly losses, underscoring just how capital-hungry the AI business is. Tellingly, around 30% of the offering was reserved for retail investors (roughly three times the norm for a mega-cap deal), with reported demand exceeding $250 billion — some 3.5 to 4 times oversubscribed. Retail enthusiasm, in short, is running hot worldwide.

What truly grips global investors, however, is what comes next. Anthropic (the developer of Claude) confidentially filed its S-1 with the SEC on June 1, at a latest valuation of roughly $965 billion — nearly double its February Series G valuation of about $380 billion, just months earlier. It is widely expected to list first and to test the trillion-dollar threshold. OpenAI (the developer of ChatGPT) is reportedly targeting a valuation of about $1 trillion and is laying the groundwork to follow. Together with SpaceX, these two form an unprecedented "trillion-dollar trio," whose combined appetite for capital is so large that analysts have warned it could disrupt global capital markets.

Their financial portraits could hardly be more different. According to multiple media reports citing unnamed sources (note: the figures below are unaudited media estimates, not official disclosures): Anthropic's annualized revenue run-rate has reportedly crossed about $47 billion, and it may be approaching its first profitable quarter — a reported ~$559 million operating profit on ~$10.9 billion of revenue for the quarter ending June 30. OpenAI, by contrast, is reported to be burning roughly $3.5 billion of cash per month and remains deeply unprofitable at the operating level. One nearing breakeven, the other spending heavily — a sharp contrast.

Here we must flag a crucial fact for investors: of these three companies, only SpaceX has publicly disclosed audited financial statements (filed on the SEC's EDGAR system); both Anthropic and OpenAI filed confidentially, and their detailed financials remain under SEC review, not yet public. Every AI-company financial figure now in circulation should therefore be treated as a reference awaiting formal verification — not as established fact.

Looking back at the railroad, telecom, and internet capital cycles, they share a single script: new technology ignites enthusiasm → capital floods in → capacity is massively overbuilt → the builders go bankrupt while the users prosper. After the telecom bubble burst, the relevant stock index plunged roughly 92%, and 25 years on it remains far below its peak. The technology did change the world — but shareholders who bet on the "builders" were often wiped out.

More striking still: by Sparkline Capital's estimates, measured as a share of GDP, today's AI capital spending already exceeds the peak of the internet boom; and once adjusted for the far shorter useful life of AI chips versus railroads, the scale of this buildout tops the chart. We identify three cracks most worth watching.

RISK ONE

The Fragile Loop of Circular Financing

The defining feature of this AI cycle is its financing structure. Capital circulates between the hyperscale cloud providers and their strategic partners: investors fund an AI company → the AI company turns around and buys compute from a cloud provider → that spending is booked as the provider's "revenue" → which lifts its valuation and underwrites still more investment. Man Group has called this one of the most intensive debt-fuelled capital cycles in modern business history, describing it vividly as a "closed, recursive financing loop."

The most emblematic case is Nvidia's commitment to invest up to $100 billion in OpenAI — capital OpenAI could in turn use to buy Nvidia chips. And this points to the delicate position of the two IPO protagonists waiting in the wings: OpenAI and Anthropic are both the largest consumers of compute within this loop, even as their spending becomes the "revenue" of the cloud and chip companies. When investors chase their IPOs, are they buying standalone earning power — or the self-reinforcing ecosystem itself? The echo of 2000, when Lucent and Nortel offered "vendor financing" to customers to inflate reported revenue, is hard to ignore. Back then, a substantial portion of those vendor-loan portfolios ultimately proved uncollectible.

Within the loop, rising valuations justify heavier capex, heavier capex is read as a signal of exploding demand, and the signal in turn reinforces valuations. The moment the real revenue curve fails to steepen in time, the loop breaks.

RISK TWO

The Hidden Cliff of Depreciation

The true economic life of a GPU may be far shorter than the accounting assumes. Many analysts argue that the hyperscalers' 5–6 year useful-life assumption for AI data centers is overly optimistic; given Nvidia's accelerating chip-replacement cadence, 2–3 years may be closer to reality.

This is no mere paper exercise. A longer useful life artificially suppresses current depreciation charges, inflates margins, and thus overstates profitability. Sparkline Capital estimates that, as capital spending converts into depreciation over time, the leading companies' annual depreciation expense could climb from roughly $150 billion to about $400 billion over the next five years, taking a real bite out of net income.

The market thus faces a double risk: on one side, "dark silicon" — idle, rapidly depreciating excess compute; on the other, profits systematically overstated by optimistic depreciation assumptions. When both surface at once, the repricing of valuations may be anything but gentle.

RISK THREE

Capital Crowding-Out and the Pricing Puzzle

The sheer density of trillion-dollar IPOs is itself a risk. SpaceX alone raised $75 billion yesterday, with OpenAI and Anthropic still to come — together, the three may raise close to $200 billion. That enormous sum has to be pulled from somewhere, and the most likely source is investors' existing "Magnificent 7" positions. In other words, even investors who never subscribe to an IPO may feel this headwind in the holdings they already own.

A deeper concern lies in who is left holding the bag. SpaceX took the unusual step of allocating about 30% of its float to retail, and retail demand worldwide has been extraordinary. The echo of history is especially clear here: when the marginal buyer of an asset shifts from institutions to an emotionally driven retail crowd, it often signals that a rally has entered its late stage — a classic lesson from the peak of the 2000 internet bubble. Feverish sentiment can push prices very high, but it also leaves them especially fragile when the mood turns.

Meanwhile, pricing itself is shrouded in doubt. Here is a coordinate worth pausing on: across the entire world, the number of publicly listed companies with a market value above $1 trillion — counting SpaceX, which debuted yesterday — is just 15. They are Nvidia, Apple, Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Tesla, Broadcom, TSMC, Saudi Aramco, Berkshire Hathaway, Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron, and SpaceX — almost all industrial titans forged over decades, with vast cash flows and real profits.

Yet OpenAI and Anthropic, still-private startups, already command private valuations approaching a threshold that only a handful of mature giants have ever reached. More to the point — because both filed their S-1s confidentially, their audited financial detail remains undisclosed to the public. Investors are, in effect, paying prices on par with the world's most elite listed companies to chase two firms whose full financial picture is not yet transparent. That gap — between valuation and visibility — is itself cause for real caution.

Take Anthropic: its valuation leapt in a matter of months — from roughly $380 billion in February 2026 to about $965 billion by May. So steep a curve leaves "fair pricing" with almost nothing to anchor to.

When new supply (the funding demands of these IPOs) rushes toward a finite pool of capital at unprecedented speed, layered atop retail-driven pricing, market fragility quietly accumulates.

What This Means for US-Equity Investors

To be clear: we do not doubt the transformative power of artificial intelligence, just as no one doubted that railroads and the internet would reshape the world. What we question is whether today's prices have already borrowed too much from that future.

History's deepest lesson is this: the real winners of a bubble are rarely the "builders," but rather the technology's "users" — and the quality businesses with genuine cash flows, sound balance sheets, and no reliance on debt transfusions. And when retail investors worldwide pour in with three times the usual allocation and unprecedented zeal, the value-investing lens of Munger and Buffett leads us to prize economic moats, capital-allocation discipline, and a clear-eyed skepticism toward the "this time is different" narrative. When others are greedy is precisely when we should keep our heads.

Recall that number once more: worldwide, only 15 listed companies have ever joined the "trillion-dollar club," most of them the tested backbone of industry. When a startup that has yet to disclose full financials is already valued alongside such giants, the investor eager to dive in should ask: am I buying business value that can withstand scrutiny — or expectations inflated by narrative and crowd sentiment?

This is not a call to avoid AI, but a call for a sufficiently thick margin of safety. We do not predict the timing — but we prepare our positions for it: holding to discipline amid the noise, and letting price, not story, decide when we act.

"Technology may well transform the world —
that does not mean any price is worth paying."
